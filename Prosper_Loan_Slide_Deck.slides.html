Effects of loan features on its APR rate¶

Investigation Overview¶

This research was started with the hopes of exploring the characteristics of loans that could be ussed to predict its APR rate i.e. the effective interest rate charge on loans.

Dataset Overview¶

The data set contained 113,937 loans with 81 variables for each loan, including loan amount, borrower rate (or interest rate), current loan status, borrower income, and many others. Variables such as LoanStatus, EmploymentStatus and Term were converted to categorical variables to aid my analysis.

Distribution of APR Rates¶

  • A loan APR is a more effective rate to consider when comparing loans. It includes not only the interest expense but also all fees and costs involved in procuring the loan.

  • The BorrowerAPR feature in the dataset was spread across a wide range from 0.65% at its least to 51%. Initially plotted with a binsize of 2, I couldn't get a good understanding of its distribution. However, the smaller binsize proved to be very insightful. The large spike at about 36% suggested that this is indeed the most common loan APR rate.

Which loan features have a significant impact on the APR?¶

BorrowerAPR VS EmploymentStatus¶

  • I used a box plot to clearly show the relationship between both features.
  • Loans borrowed by those receiving some form of income (i.e Employed, Full-time, Other, Part-time, Retired and Self-employed) have a lower interest rate compared to borrowers who are not employed they're more likely to pay back compared to the loans borrowed by those who are unemployed.
  • The interest rates for those who are self-employed were also higher compared to those in full-time jobs due to job security.

BorrowerAPR VS ListingCategory¶

  • I used a violin plot to clearly show the relationship between both features.

  • The diagram below shows that loans taken for student use had one of the highest interest rates. Loans to students can be quite risky because most of them don't have a steady form of income to repay back the loans. These high rates are pput in place to compensate the investors for the level of risk. However, the other end of the violin plot shows that some loans taken for student use also had low interest rates. This might be students doing a masters degree paid for by companies. In this case, they already have jobs they can go back to and therefore the level of risk is much lower.

  • Green loans are loans taken out for the sole purpose of supporting and investing in environmentally sustainable economic activities. I believe the interest rates for these are quite high because it is a fairly new type of investment hence companies such as Prosper loans aren't quite sure about how to assess their chances of success just yet. It makes sense that they'll rather be pessimistic in their assessments than sanguine.

ListingCategory VS EmploymentStatus¶

  • A heatmap was best to show the relationship between these two categorical variables. The heatmap counts the number loans associated with these categorical variables.
  • The debt consolidation column seemed to have the highest count for each employment status. This shouldn't come as a surprise becase as we saw in the 'ListingCategory', it was the most popular reason for taking out loans.
  • Home improvement is another common reason borrowers take out loans and this was very popular among the employed and full-time workers. However, the Self-employed workers seemed to be more interested in business than Home improvement. This makes sense because most self-employed workers don't have a large enough team to pool resources and make investments, hence they result to borrowing from lenders.
  • One very important thing to note here is that the category 'Employed' under EmploymentStatus is a very broad term. Borrowers classed as 'Employed' could be as well be full-time, part-time or self-employed. Hence, to get better insights, I'll be excluded the 'Employed' category from the rest of the analysis.
  • I also excluded the 'Other' and 'Not Available' categories under ListingCategory because these were irrelevant.

How do EmploymentStatus and ListingCategory jointly affect the BorrowerAPR?¶

BorrowerAPR by EmploymentStatus and ListingCategory¶

  • Generally, we see that loans taken out by the not employed workers have higher interest rates compared to the loans taken out by the full-time workers. However, there are some interesting insights that the listing category highlights:

  • Loans taken out for business use by the full-time workers have relatively low interest rates of 20%. The average interest rate for loans taken out by unemployed workers on the other hand is about 29%. I believe this is because if the business investment fails, the unemployed worker has nothing to fall back on unlike the full-time workers who still have their primary jobs to fall back on.

  • Particularly, loans taken out by unemployed workers for the Home Improvement and Household expenses appear to have pretty high interest rates. I believe this is because these arent investments in any form coupled with the fact that these borrowers have no form of regular income. It is also not possible to use these assets as collateral because the second they are in use, its value depreciates.

  • Loans taken out by part-time and retired borrowers as Personal loans have some of the lowest interest rates of about 15% and 18% respectively. These are loans that arent secured against any asset such as a home (i.e. they have no collateral). Its not quite clear why Prosper Loans will charge such low rates for these loans but I reckon it is because historically, the default rates for 'personal loans' taken by the part-time and retired borrowers have been pretty low.
